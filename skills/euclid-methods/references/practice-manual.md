# Euclidean Thinking — Practice Manual

A practical guide to internalising the six cognitive habits from Euclid's *Elements*.

---

## 1. What This Manual Is

This is not a summary of the talk. It's a **training protocol** — how to take the six cognitive habits and make them reflexive. The skill (`euclid-methods`) gives you the framework and exercises. This manual tells you *how to practise, how to progress, and how to know you're improving*.

---

## 2. The Three Learning Phases

### Phase 1: Recognition (Weeks 1–2)

**Goal:** Spot violations in the wild. You're not fixing anything yet — just noticing.

**Practice:**
- Each day, pick **one habit** from the six. Just one.
- Throughout the day, notice every time someone (including you) violates it in conversation, email, or writing.
- At end of day, log: *"I noticed [habit] was violated when [situation]. The violation was [specific behaviour]."*

**Target:** 3–5 sightings per day by week 2.

**Example (Habit 1 — Define Terms):**
> *"In the team meeting, Sarah said 'we need more efficiency' but nobody asked what she meant. I noticed it because efficiency could mean cost reduction, time reduction, or fewer handoffs — all different things. I didn't flag it yet, but I saw it."*

**Why this phase first:** You can't fix a habit you can't see. Most people spend their whole lives not noticing they're arguing by assertion. Phase 1 builds the radar.

---

### Phase 2: Intervention (Weeks 3–6)

**Goal:** Stop yourself mid-violation. Then start rewriting.

**Practice:**
- Keep the same daily habit pick.
- When you catch yourself violating a habit in real time (in an email, a meeting comment, a text), stop. Pause.
- **Rewrite** the utterance the Euclidean way before sending/speaking.
- If you can't rewrite it in the moment, write the corrected version within 1 hour.

**Target:** 2–3 successful real-time corrections per day by week 4.

**Progression:** Move from after-the-fact corrections (week 3) to before-you-say-it corrections (week 6).

**Example (Habit 4 — Cite the Rule):**
> *Before: "Obviously, this policy will reduce costs because it streamlines approvals."*
> *After: "This policy will reduce costs [claim]. The reason [rule]: approval steps drop from 5 to 2 [evidence]. By common notion one, fewer steps means fewer hours billed [logic chain]."*

---

### Phase 3: Automaticity (Weeks 7–12)

**Goal:** The habits operate below conscious thought for routine arguments. You only consciously invoke the framework for high-stakes decisions.

**Practice:**
- Drop the daily habit pick. Instead, run the **5-minute morning drill** from the skill (SKILL.md) as a warm-up.
- For any argument that matters (negotiation, policy submission, investment decision, biblical interpretation debate), **write it out in full Euclidean form** before acting.
- Review one old argument per week through the Euclidean lens.

**Target:** You no longer think "I should use the Euclid method" — you just find yourself defining terms before arguing. That's automaticity.

## 3. Situation-Specific Protocols

### Using It for Policy Work (APS)

Policy documents are arguments disguised as reports. Apply the framework:

| Policy Question | Euclidean Move |
|---|---|
| "What does this term mean?" | Habit 1 — Define terms. Policy documents are full of undefined abstractions (efficiency, equity, sustainability, wellbeing). Before evaluating an option, demand a definition. |
| "Why do we think this will work?" | Habit 2 — State assumptions. Every policy recommendation rests on causal assumptions (X → Y). Pull them into the open. |
| "Is this conclusion justified by the evidence?" | Habit 4 — Cite the rule. What's the rule connecting evidence to conclusion? "We have data that X → we should do Y" — what's the missing rule that moves from data to action? |
| "Are there alternatives we're not considering?" | Habit 6 — Assume the opposite. Write the case for *not* doing the recommended policy. Does it hold? If not, why not? |

**Practical workflow:** Before submitting a policy brief, run it through these four questions. Flag any you can't answer clearly.

---

### Using It for Biblical Study (Sola Scriptura)

The Euclidean method aligns naturally with Berean verification (Acts 17:11 — "they examined the Scriptures daily to see if these things were so").

| Biblical Reasoning | Euclidean Move |
|---|---|
| "What does this word mean in the original language?" | Habit 1 — Define terms. Don't import modern definitions into Koine Greek or Biblical Hebrew. |
| "What assumptions am I bringing to this text?" | Habit 2 — State assumptions. Every reader has a theological grid. Name yours before you interpret. |
| "Does my conclusion follow from the text, or am I asserting it?" | Habit 3 — Construct, don't assert. The text must earn the conclusion. |
| "Does my interpretation contradict clear passages?" | Habit 6 — Assume the opposite. If your interpretation is correct, what would the alternative interpretation require? Does that lead to a contradiction with Scripture? |

**Warning:** Reductio ad absurdum does not apply to divine revelation itself. It applies to *your reasoning about* revelation. The method checks your logic, not God's truth.

---

### Using It for Financial Decisions (Investing)

| Investment Question | Euclidean Move |
|---|---|
| "What do I mean by 'good investment'?" | Habit 1 — Define terms. Capital growth? Yield? Inflation hedge? Tax efficiency? |
| "What are my assumptions about the market?" | Habit 2 — State assumptions. "Property always appreciates," "Bitcoin is digital gold," "The ASX returns 8% pa." These are axioms, not facts. State them. |
| "Why does this property/asset meet my criteria?" | Habit 4 — Cite the rule. The chain from criteria → evidence → conclusion must be explicit. |
| "What would have to be true for this to fail?" | Habit 6 — Assume the opposite. Stress-test every investment thesis by assuming it's wrong and following the consequences. |

**Practical workflow:** Before any investment above $1K, write a one-page Euclidean case. Define your terms. State your assumptions. Build the chain from evidence to conclusion. Then write the opposite case.

---

### Using It for Negotiation / Meetings

| Meeting Situation | Euclidean Move |
|---|---|
| Someone says "we need X" without defining it | Habit 1 — "Just to clarify, what do you mean by X?" |
| Someone says "clearly/obviously" | Habit 3 — "Could you walk me through why that follows?" |
| Decision feels forced with no alternatives | Habit 6 — "Let me play devil's advocate — what would happen if we did the opposite?" |
| Multiple options on the table | Habit 5 — "What's the dependency chain here? What has to be solved first?" |

## 4. Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Using the Framework as a Cudgel

**The mistake:** Demanding definitions and proofs from everyone in every conversation. You'll come across as pedantic and insufferable.

**The fix:** Use the framework on *your own* thinking first. Only externally deploy it in high-stakes situations or with consent ("Mind if I pressure-test your argument?"). Internally, always have it running.

### Pitfall 2: Mistaking Rigour for Rightness

**The mistake:** Because your argument is structured Euclidean-style, you assume it's correct.

**The fix:** Sound structure doesn't guarantee true premises. Garbage axioms → beautiful proofs → garbage conclusions. Check your axioms separately.

### Pitfall 3: Skipping Phase 1

**The mistake:** Jumping straight to the exercises (Phase 2) without building the recognition radar first.

**The fix:** You can't fix what you can't see. Spend two weeks on Phase 1 even if it feels slow. Most people who abandon this framework skipped recognition.

### Pitfall 4: Trying All Six Habits at Once

**The mistake:** Attempting to practise all six habits simultaneously.

**The fix:** One habit per day. Rotate weekly. After 6 weeks you've cycled through each one 7 times. That's the minimum for neural rewiring.

### Pitfall 5: Neglecting Habit 2 (State Assumptions)

**The mistake:** Doing the exercises but skipping the step where you write down your axioms.

**The fix:** This is the hardest habit and the most valuable. Most arguments collapse not on logic but on hidden assumptions. Force yourself to write assumptions down. It feels unnatural because it is — that's exactly why it matters.

## 5. Self-Assessment

### Weekly Check (End of Week)

Rate yourself 1–5 on each:

| Question | 1 | 5 |
|---|---|---|
| Did I notice violations of my target habit? | Saw none | Saw 5+ |
| Did I correct myself mid-argument? | Corrected after | Corrected before |
| Did I write out any argument in full Euclidean form? | None | 3+ times |
| Did I identify someone else's hidden assumption? | Never | Multiple times |
| Did I use reductio to test a belief? | Never | Yes, on a real belief |

### Milestone Markers

| Milestone | Signal |
|---|---|
| Phase 1 complete | You notice undefined terms in casual conversation automatically |
| Phase 2 complete | You pause before using "clearly" or "obviously" in writing |
| Phase 3 complete | You find yourself structuring arguments backwards (conclusion → dependencies → axioms) without trying |
| Mastery | You can spot a gap in someone's argument within 30 seconds and articulate *why* it's a gap |

---

## 6. Advanced Practice

Once the six habits are automatic, push further:

**Cross-domain transfer:** Apply the framework to domains where it seems unnatural — creative work, relationships, prayer. *What assumptions am I making about this relationship? What would it mean to "prove" a creative intuition? What are the axioms of my prayer life?*

**Teach it:** Explain the framework to someone else (Patience, a colleague, a soldier in your unit). Teaching forces you to clarify your own understanding.

**Combine with the metacognition radar:** Load `meta-cognition` alongside `euclid-methods`. The metacognition radar catches when you're in cognitive distortion; the Euclidean framework gives you the tool to correct it.
