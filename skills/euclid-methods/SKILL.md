# Euclid's Six Cognitive Habits — Mastery Skill

Load this skill when you want to **practice or apply** the rigorous thinking methods from Euclid's *Elements* — the 2300-year-old geometry textbook that reshaped Lincoln, Hobbes, Einstein, and Russell. None of them became geometers. All of them learned **how to think from first principles**.

For the full story context (the case studies and why these habits work), see the [curated transcript](references/curated-transcript.md).

## The Six Habits (Quick Reference Card)

| # | Habit | The Rule | Think Like |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Define your terms** | Before arguing, say exactly what you mean. | Russell — assumptions in plain sight |
| 2 | **State your assumptions** | Put axioms up front. Every system has them. | Russell — intellectual honesty |
| 3 | **Construct, don't assert** | Every conclusion earned. No leaps. No "obviously." | Lincoln — cite the rule |
| 4 | **Cite the rule at every step** | Name what justifies each move. | Lincoln — demonstrate or don't |
| 5 | **Break complex problems down** | Solve dependencies first. Build backwards. | Einstein — dependency chain |
| 6 | **Assume the opposite** | Reductio ad absurdum. Follow negation to contradiction. | Hobbes — prove by impossibility |

## The Three Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Three-Layer Argument (Lincoln)

> **Use when:** You need to make a persuasive argument — negotiation, meeting, piece of writing.

1. **Define your key terms precisely.** Write down what you mean by each loaded word: success, efficiency, fairness, risk, value.
2. **State your assumptions explicitly.** What are you taking as given without proving it?
3. **For every move from one claim to the next, name the rule you're applying.** If you can't name it, your thinking isn't clear enough yet.

**Practice prompt:** Take a current decision you're facing (a purchase, a hire, a policy choice). Write the case for your position in three layers above. Then hand it to someone and ask: *"Where's the gap?"*

---

### Exercise 2: Prove by Assuming the Opposite (Hobbes / Reductio)

> **Use when:** You believe something is true but struggle to demonstrate it directly.

1. Write down the **negation** (opposite claim) at the top of a page.
2. Follow that opposite claim as far as it will take you:
   - *What would have to be true if the opposite were true?*
   - *What would that imply?*
   - *And what would that then imply?*
3. Keep following the chain until you either:
   - Reach a **contradiction** — something that violates what you already know → your original is proven.
   - Realize your original belief was **shakier than you thought** → you've discovered a blind spot.

**Practice prompt:** Pick a strongly held belief you hold (political, financial, strategic). Write its opposite at the top of a fresh page. Commit. Follow the chain. Report what you find.

---

### Exercise 3: Backward Dependency Mapping (Einstein)

> **Use when:** You face a complex problem that feels overwhelming.

1. Do NOT try to solve it in one move.
2. Ask: *What smaller problems must I solve first for the larger solution to be possible?*
3. Build the dependency chain **backwards**:
   - *What must be true for X to work?*
   - *What must be true for that to work?*
4. Keep going until you reach claims simple enough to **verify directly**.
5. Then build forward from there.

**Practice prompt:** Take a long-term goal (financial independence, a career move, a fitness target). Map the backward dependency chain. What are the simplest verifiable claims at the bottom?

## Practice Protocol (Daily Drill)

To internalise these habits, run this 5-minute drill each morning:

| Minute | Activity |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Habit pick.** Choose one of the six habits you want to strengthen. |
| 2 | **Identify a gap.** Find one argument you made yesterday where you violated that habit (asserted without citing, used a fuzzy term, hid an assumption). |
| 3 | **Rewrite it.** Express the same argument the Euclidean way — terms defined, assumptions stated, rules cited. |
| 4 | **Apply it today.** Identify one decision or conversation coming up today where you can use that habit deliberately. |
| 5 | **Log it.** Write one sentence: "Today I will practice [habit] by [specific action]." |
